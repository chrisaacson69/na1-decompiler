ai_per_fief_command_driver

$B89B (bank 1) — how the AI plays each fief’s turn.
① decompiler → C ② data-walk labels ③ bytecode-verified flow ④ emulator-probed formula

The planner ticks the world and the shuffle decides who moves; this is where each daimyo actually plays. ai_per_fief_command_driver walks the turn order and, for every fief, dispatches on one number — province_ai_state, the Grant policy the governing daimyo was assigned (0–5). That number selects one of six handlers, each biased toward a different command. Here’s the twist we confirmed by save-state census: those six policies are a player affordance — a delegation menu so you don’t micromanage. The AI never uses them. Every AI fief sits permanently at state 0 (Home), which routes to the RNG-weighted Balanced loop. So the handlers below describe the full Grant menu, but the enemy only ever runs one of them. They share a small kit of economic helpers and a single war routine, and they all spend against a budget that grows with the game year. We re-walked the whole pass against the post-fix decompile (the earlier notes were stale) and asked the ROM directly — both which enemy the AI attacks (overturning one of our labels) and whether the AI picks a style at all (it doesn’t).

The steps

The command pass, fief by fief — every stage grounded in the bytecode of ai_per_fief_command_driver $B89B:

  1. $B8B1Per-season turn flag. Clear ai_turn_flags bit 0, then set it iff rng%100 ≥ 55 − skill×5 — a difficulty-scaled coin-flip (skill 1 ≈ 50%, skill 5 ≈ 70%) that gates this season’s merchant/trade availability.
  2. $B90EPick the fief. selected_province_idx = daimyo_turn_order[i] — the shuffled order from phase 4. The redraw shows “Turn %d / fief %d”.
  3. $B943Resting? If rest_turns_remaining[fief] is non-zero the daimyo is recovering (illness / post-battle) and the turn is skipped.
  4. $B94CDispatch on the Grant policy. switch (province_ai_state[fief]) → one of six handlers (table below). This single byte is the AI’s entire “personality” for the fief.
  5. $B989Execute & show. issue_province_command commits the chosen command and draws it. Player-controlled fiefs (state 5 / Direct) reach the interactive command menu here instead; AI fiefs just display what the handler already did.
  6. $B996Resume marker. The loop index is persisted to ai_per_fief_loop_index $6001 so a war declared mid-pass can drop into the bank-2 combat engine and the pass can resume at the next fief afterward — the keystone the spine page calls out.

The six Grant policies — the player’s delegation menu

Each policy is a handler with a different command bias. They are not hard scripts — every one leans on RNG and the fief’s resource surplus — but the lean is real. These are what the player picks with the Grant command to auto-run a fief; the enemy AI uses only state 0 (see the census below):

stateGrant policyHandlerWhat the fief does
0Homeai_econ_action_state0 $B875General econ dispatch + a random daimyo-stat nudge; ~25% chance it also bumps arms.
1Industrialai_develop_town_handler $B3ABCommerce: convert gold↔rice toward balance, ship surplus to other fiefs, then grow.
2Militaryai_state2_recruit_arm_train $B4D5Try war first (below); otherwise hire men, buy arms, and train.
3Balancedai_econ_command_dispatch $B64BThe general dispatcher — an RNG-weighted pick across recruit / town / dam-and-grow.
4Farmingai_develop_dam_and_grow $B42BAgriculture: build dams while output < ceiling, then grow output.
5Direct— (interactive menu)Player-controlled (or fully manual) — no AI handler; issue_province_command opens the menu.

State 0 (Home) is the important one: it calls ai_econ_command_dispatch — the same routine as state 3 (Balanced) — so the AI’s universal policy is the all-around probabilistic mix that can recruit, develop, or go to war on any given turn.

Does the AI pick a style? No — it’s always Home

The dispatch reads province_ai_state[fief] fresh every turn, so whatever is stored is what runs. We took a census of that byte across every save dump (tools/probe-ai-policy-distribution.py) — and the AI never varies:

saveprovince_ai_state histogram
every ordinary mid-game saveHome ×49, Direct ×1
blockade-complete (player owns ~7)Home ×37, Military ×1, Balanced ×5, Direct ×7
post-victory (player owns ~17)Home ×33, Balanced ×10, Direct ×7

Every AI fief sits at 0 (Home); the lone Direct (5) is the human’s own fief. States 1–4 show up only in saves where the player has conquered and granted policies to their own fiefs — and they scale with how much the player owns. New-game seeds all fiefs to 0 ($89F9); character-creation stamps the player fief to 5; conquered AI fiefs stay 0. So the “six personalities” are entirely a player convenience. The enemy is monomorphic: it runs the Home / Balanced dispatcher on every fief, and the variety you see between AI fiefs comes from their differing resource surplus, RNG, and daimyo stats — never from a chosen policy.

How a fief decides what it can afford

Before spending, every econ handler computes a surplus with ai_calc_men_surplus_over_gold_and_rice $B2EF: gold − men and rice − men, both floored at 0. In this engine a fief’s standing army size is the unit of account — you only have “spare” gold or rice once it exceeds the men you must feed. That surplus is what gets shipped, grown, or spent on troops.

And the spending ceilings scale with the calendar. Every cap is of the form (current_game_year − 1559) × N, so an AI fief in 1582 may commit far more per command than the same fief in 1560:

HandlerCap (per command)
Industrial — ship goldmin(ceiling, (year−1559)×100 + 100)

Military — hire menmin(ceiling, (year−1559)×100)
Military — buy armsmin(ceiling, (year−1559)×40)

This is a deliberate ramp: the AI plays small early and escalates, so the late game gets more aggressive on its own even before territory snowballs.

The war decision

The militarist handler opens with ai_try_war_attack $949A. It is a filter, not a gamble — most turns it returns “no war” at one of three gates, and the rest pick a target carefully:

  1. $94B3Self-gate. Bail if the attacker’s own daimyo carries the daimyo_weakness_flag (a low-health mark set by the planner), or has no men, or no gold.
  2. $DAD7Build the candidate list. combat_helper_dad7 seeds deduped_owner_list $6F4F from the fief’s adjacency table ($8004, ≤4 neighbours), then combat_helper_dd3a keeps only the active, enemy-owned ones. The AI can only attack a neighbour — geography is hard-coded into the target set.
  3. $93B3Default target = the weakest neighbour. pick_weakest_men_fief returns the adjacent enemy with the fewest provisioned men (see the ROM check below). A fief that is out of rice counts as zero men (fief_men_if_provisioned $9382) — starve a fief and the AI reads it as defenceless.
  4. $945DGrudge override. If a designated rival ($6E7F) owns an adjacent fief weaker than the attacker, that fief becomes the target instead.
  5. $9423Diplomatic pick. Among targets the attacker isn’t at good relations with — and only if its daimyo’s Drive ≥ 50 — it again prefers the weakest; a roughly-matched such target can replace the default.
  6. $90BBCommit gate. ai_commit_attack_deduct_resources musters ≈ 60–89% of min(gold, men, rice×2) and attacks only if that force ≥ the defender’s weaker of {men, rice}. So target selection is weakest-prey, but the final go/no-go is a genuine strength check — the AI rarely throws itself at a fief it can’t out-muster on at least one axis.

Asking the ROM: which enemy does it attack?

The target-pickers all compare armies through fief_men_ratio_pct, and the decompiled C displays its arguments reverse-pushed — so reading the C naively inverts every “who’s bigger” test. Rather than hand-trace the VM’s frame ABI, we ran the picker in the emulator (tools/probe-ai-war-target.py): a synthetic candidate list of three fiefs with known army sizes, executed straight off the ROM.

candidate fiefs (men)fief returnedverdict
200, 50, 120the 50 fieffewest men
50, 200, 120 (reordered)the 50 fieffewest men

The routine returns the weakest adjacent enemy in both orderings — so our own label pick_fief_with_most_men was backwards. Corrected to pick_weakest_men_fief in mesen-labels.toml (and the reverse-push direction is now annotated on fief_men_ratio_pct $939D for every other caller). This both confirms the old “attacks the weakest neighbour” intuition and fixes the code that says so.

What it pinned down

The Grant policies are a player tool; the AI is monomorphic. province_ai_state (0–5) selects the per-fief handler, but a save-state census (probe-ai-policy-distribution.py) shows every AI fief permanently at 0 (Home) — states 1–4 appear only where the player granted them. The enemy doesn’t pick a style; it always runs Home, which is the RNG-weighted Balanced dispatcher (ai_econ_action_state0 → ai_econ_command_dispatch). Per-fief variety is resource state + RNG + daimyo stats, not policy.
The AI plays bigger as the years pass. Every spending cap is (year − 1559) × N. The opponent escalates on a calendar ramp, independent of how much land it holds — a built-in difficulty curve over time.
